## Professional Self-Assessment

Completing the Computer Science program and building this ePortfolio helped me see my work in a more connected and professional way. When I first started the program, I mostly wanted a degree that would help me get into technology and open up better opportunities for my family. Over time, that became more specific. I still value software development, but my interests have shifted toward practical and secure systems work, especially in areas like healthcare, government, and cybersecurity-related roles. Putting this portfolio together forced me to look back at the work I completed throughout the program and ask a more professional question, which was not just whether I finished an assignment, but what that work actually says about the kind of computer science professional I am becoming.

One of the biggest things I gained from the program was a better understanding of how technical work connects to real people and real organizations. That shows up in how I now think about communication, collaboration, and stakeholder needs. In several courses, I had to write as if I were explaining a technical decision to someone nontechnical, whether that was a client, a manager, or another stakeholder. That skill became even more important in this capstone because the portfolio itself is really an exercise in communication. It is not enough to build something that works. I also have to explain why it works, why it matters, what tradeoffs I made, and how it fits a real use case. I also learned that collaboration is not just about working in a group project. It is also about writing readable code, organizing work clearly, documenting changes, and making technical decisions in ways that support other people’s understanding and decision-making.

Technically speaking, one of the most powerful themes throughout my curriculum thus far has been understanding the relationships among components within computer science. Data structures and algorithms gave me an appreciation for logic, ordering, efficiency, and the handling of data. Software engineering and design gave me the perspective of thinking in terms of architecture, maintenance, scope, and iteration. Working with databases made clear that there is far more to consider when dealing with data storage than simply the storage itself. The concept of designing a reliable system goes far beyond just being able to store the data; it includes verifying it, formatting it, and making it useful long-term. Another one of the disciplines that I have come to appreciate more since I started my studies here is security. When I first began my studies, security seemed to be a separate entity altogether. However, now that I am more experienced, I see it as something that should govern my entire approach.

This portfolio reflects that growth through one core artifact that I enhanced across three categories. I started with my CS 465 full-stack project, which originally was a travel-based application called Travlr Getaways. For the software design and engineering enhancement, I repurposed that artifact into the first version of SecureRastrear, a more professional incident-tracking system. That change showed my ability to adapt an existing architecture to a new problem and improve the design in a way that better matched my current career direction. In terms of the algorithms and data structures enhancement, I developed priority scoring, number of days open calculations, searches, filters, sorts, and metrics that would make the incident list act as a more robust triage and decision-making tool. With regards to the database enhancement, I improved the incident schema, developed validation and indexing capability, and implemented auditing of create, update, and delete actions. When considering all three of these enhancements, the connection between quality software development and good problem-solving became clear to me. Namely, the greatest code is that which is not only functional but also well thought-out and well-structured as far as solving the underlying problem is concerned. Throughout this portfolio, my main focus was on creating an application capable of catering to multiple stakeholders, from technical personnel to administrators to decision makers.

This ePortfolio also helped me better define my professional goals and values. I want to be someone who builds or supports systems that are useful, secure, and grounded in real operational needs. The focus has shifted from pursuing prestige to ensuring stability, value, and the production of useful and practical technical solutions. Moreover, the desire for work that is informed by the principle of security has emerged. This portfolio will help to prove that, especially should there be a deeper interest in working in healthcare, governmental agencies, or cyber security domains. In other words, the artifacts included demonstrate that I have a capacity to enhance an existing system, apply logical reasoning and prioritization, enhance data management, and justify the rationale behind my actions.

To conclude, the Computer Science degree program allowed me to evolve from an inexperienced person who had no clear idea of what he wanted to become to an individual who developed a more definite vision of the type of professional activities he wishes to pursue in the future. While the artifacts provided in the portfolio are far from ideal, they allow illustrating considerable improvement in terms of developing technical competence, communication skills, and even the ability to reason like a mature computer science specialist.

## Portfolio Overview

This portfolio is built around one core artifact from **CS 465 Full Stack Development I**, enhanced across three computer science categories:

- **Software Design and Engineering**
- **Algorithms and Data Structures**
- **Databases**

The artifact began as **Travlr Getaways** and was repurposed into **SecureRastrear**, an incident-tracking system. Across the three enhancements, I improved the software architecture, added triage and prioritization logic, and strengthened the database layer with schema validation, indexing, and audit-history tracking.
